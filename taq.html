The Alternate
Quest

A hiring platform that replaced resumes with real work, built to connect students to paid remote internships through startup problems.

Project Overview

The Alternate Quest started from a simple observation: resumes tell you what someone has done, not what they can do. For students with no work history, the resume is an almost useless document. And for early-stage startups looking for sharp, motivated collaborators, filtering by GPA and club memberships misses the point entirely.

TAQ proposed a different system. Instead of applications, students were assigned real startup problems. What they shipped in response became their candidacy. The work itself was the pitch.

The project was incubated at NSRCEL IIMB from 2024 to 2025. In its first cohort, run over two months, TAQ secured paid remote jobs for full-time students, with no commute requirement and no experience prerequisite beyond the ability to show up and build.

The Problem

For students

Entry-level roles increasingly ask for experience. Students in full-time degree programmes rarely have the time or access to build a traditional CV. The standard advice, "do internships to get internships," is circular and excludes those who need it most.

For startups

Early-stage teams need people who can operate with ambiguity and actually ship things. Resume screening selects for polish, not capability. By the time a startup finds someone useful through conventional hiring, they've spent weeks on a process that wasn't designed for them.

"Instead of resumes, TAQ focused on what students built and shipped."

The Approach

Problem-based candidacy

Startup partners submitted real, scoped problems. Students were given these as their entry point. The submission wasn't a cover letter or a grade, it was a working solution, a prototype, a strategy deck, or a research output depending on the brief.

Remote and paid by design

Both conditions were non-negotiable from the start. Remote meant students in any city could participate without uprooting their academic schedule. Paid meant the work was treated as real labour, not a favour students owed to their career prospects. The model only worked if both sides had genuine stakes.

Incubation at NSRCEL IIMB

Being incubated at NSRCEL gave TAQ credibility with startup partners and access to a network that took the project seriously as an operational experiment rather than a student initiative. The institutional anchor mattered for the first cohort, where the model had not yet proven itself through outcomes.
